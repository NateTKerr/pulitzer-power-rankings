Analysis

Newer Books Are Better

There's a -0.56 correlation between publication year and where I ranked each book.

Most Pre-1970s Winners Have Been Completely Forgotten

70% of 1930s winners are classified as "Forgotten" in my dataset. That number drops to zero for every decade after 2000.

The First 51 Years Were All White Authors

From the prize's founding in 1918 through 1968, every single winner was white. N. Scott Momaday broke the streak in 1969. The 2020s are the first decade where non-white winners outnumber white ones (4 of 7).

But Non-White Authors Ranked Higher

White authors average rank 52. Non-white authors average rank 33.

Women Were There Early, Got Pushed Out, and Are Still Fighting Back

Women won more than half the prizes in the 1920s and 1930s, then they didn't win a single one between 1942 and 1961. The 2010s are still 7-to-2 in favor of men and women haven't won more than men in any decade since the 1930s.

Literary Risk-Taking Gets Rewarded (If It's the Right Kind)

Experimental fiction averages rank 33. Regional and genre fiction averages 68. Modernism, despite its reputation, averages 71, mostly because its winners are from early decades nobody reads anymore.

A Third of All Winners Are Set in the South

34 of 99 books have Southern settings. That's a third of the entire list.

Winning Twice Doesn't Mean Both Books Are Great

Faulkner's two wins sit at #78 and #96. Tarkington's land at #88 and #89. Winning the Pulitzer twice is a career achievement, but it doesn't guarantee both books will hold up.

Bigger Books, Higher Tiers (Mostly)

The top three tiers average around 420–465 pages. The bottom four tiers average 320–399. The relationship isn't perfectly linear, but the rankings do tilt toward books with more scope.
